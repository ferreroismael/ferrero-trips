The diary of a family that can't sit still. Six people, thirty countries, and the ongoing conviction that the best stories happen when you leave the plan at home.

↓ Scroll to read

We are Ismael, Malena, Lara, Amelie, and Taty — a family split between Argentina and Norway, which means we’re always either jet-lagged, homesick, or both. This is not a highlights reel. It’s the real thing: wrong turns into something magnificent, cheap noodles that tasted like the best meal of our lives, and all the moments in between that remind us why we keep going.

Chapter 01

Asia
2025

Japan · Singapore · Thailand Spring 2025 1,955 Photos

The bullet train from Tokyo to Kyoto is everything Japan promises you before you arrive: impossibly punctual, impossibly quiet, impossibly fast — a country passing in silence at 300 km/h while Amelie pressed her nose against the window and refused to blink. We had explained the Shinkansen three times at home. None of it prepared her for the actual thing. That gap, between expectation and reality, turned out to be the defining texture of this entire trip.

Japan does something unusual to children. The deer in Nara — wild animals wandering among ancient cedar trees — bowed at the girls for crackers. "The deer are polite," Lara concluded, with the kind of gravity seven-year-olds reserve for major discoveries. We had been in the country four days and had already stopped trying to explain things. Better to just walk and let the place work on everyone.

Kyoto in April is a cliché for a reason. The sakura crowds are real — shoulder to shoulder along the Philosopher's Path, everyone photographing the same blossoms. But step three streets back, into a neighbourhood with no English signage, and suddenly you’re the only family eating yakitori in a seven-seat counter restaurant where the chef speaks no Spanish and we spoke no Japanese, and between Google Translate and a lot of pointing, we somehow ordered the best meal of the trip. The girls ate everything. The chef seemed pleased. Malena cried a little. It was a Tuesday.

Singapore for four days felt like pressing fast-forward. The city is relentless in the best possible way — it wants you fed, dazzled, and slightly confused about what country you’re in. The hawker centres near Maxwell Road became our morning ritual: kaya toast and soft-boiled eggs at a plastic table while the heat built around us. The girls learned that breakfast could be an event rather than an interruption to the morning.

Gardens by the Bay lit up at night while a choir of steel supertrees synchronized to music. The girls danced. We filmed it badly. We will watch that video for the rest of our lives.

Thailand was the deceleration the trip needed. After the precision of Japan and the velocity of Singapore, arriving in Chiang Mai felt like someone had turned the contrast down gently. The night markets, the temples draped in orange light, and a guesthouse family that adopted us for a week, leaving fruit outside our door each morning without explanation. "I want to come back when I’m big," Amelie said on the last night — which is the highest praise a five-year-old can give anything.

Chapter 02

Japan
2024

Tokyo · Osaka · Hiroshima 2024 380 Photos

The first rule of going to Japan is that once is never enough. The second rule is that you will believe, before the second trip, that you already know what to expect. You do not. Japan has a talent for revealing new layers every time — different neighbourhoods, different seasons, different versions of the girls encountering the same city with older eyes.

This was the trip where Lara discovered Japanese convenience stores as a personal philosophy. 7-Eleven in Japan is not 7-Eleven anywhere else: it’s a warm universe of onigiri, tamagoyaki sandwiches, perfect coffee in tiny cans, and seasonal snacks you’ve never seen before and will spend the next year trying to recreate at home.

Hiroshima is not a place you visit lightly. We walked through the Peace Memorial Museum slowly, without phones, the girls old enough to understand that something enormous had happened here and young enough to feel the weight of it without needing it explained. Some places don’t need a caption. They just need you to be present.

Osaka gave us permission to eat irresponsibly. Takoyaki in the street at 11pm. Okonomiyaki the size of a wheel, cooked at the table while the chef watched our technique with polite concern. Kushikatsu on every corner — the girls declared it the greatest food innovation since pizza. Malena, who is from Buenos Aires and takes food seriously in ways that are somewhat competitive, admitted it might be the best fried food she had ever eaten. This is not a small statement.

Japan in 2024 was different from Japan in 2025 in ways that are hard to articulate but easy to feel. The girls were a year younger, the city the same, but we walked through it differently — more slowly, more willing to sit somewhere unremarkable for an hour and just watch. Japan rewards that kind of attention.

Chapter 03

México
2022

Ciudad de México · Oaxaca · Yucatán 2022 1,856 Photos

Mexico is where the whole family makes sense. Between Ismael’s Argentine directness and the country’s baseline warmth, between Malena’s instinct for finding the best street food in any city and the fact that Mexican street food is objectively the best street food in the world — the whole thing clicked from day one. We hit the ground running in Mexico City and did not stop for three weeks.

CDMX first, because there is no version of Mexico that doesn’t start in the capital. The sheer weight of history layered into the city — Aztec ruins beneath Spanish colonial plazas, modernist murals on government buildings — is staggering in the best possible way. Taty found Diego Rivera’s murals in the National Palace and stood for forty minutes reading every panel. The girls climbed on things they probably weren’t supposed to climb on.

Oaxaca broke us open in the way only a city that smells of chocolate and mezcal can. The mole negro alone — a sauce that takes days to make, that contains everything — is worth a ten-hour flight. We ate it three days in a row. We took a cooking class where the instructor laughed at Ismael’s grinding technique and was not wrong to do so.

The Yucatán Peninsula exists in a different register. The heat is absolute. The colour of the Caribbean water is not a real colour — it looks edited, like someone ran a filter that bumped the teal saturation 40% above what nature is supposed to produce. Lara swam for six hours straight. Amelie collected shells with the methodical intensity she applies to everything.

Chichén Itzá at 7am, before the tour buses, is close to what the ancient world might have felt like. The Pyramid of Kukulcán casting its shadow, the acoustics of the ball court where a handclap produces nine echoes — whoever designed these spaces understood something about scale and human attention that most modern architects have forgotten.

The cenotes turned out to be the highlight the girls talked about for the rest of the year. Jumping from a platform twelve metres up, surfacing into green filtered light, roots reaching down from the jungle above — it’s the kind of thing that recalibrates your sense of what the world can look like.

Chapter 04

Norway
by Road

Fjords · Mountains · Coast Summer 2020 633 Photos

The world was shutting down in the spring of 2020, and renting a motorhome and driving into the Norwegian wilderness was either an act of wisdom or controlled madness — we still debate which. Six people, one vehicle, and a country of fjords and silence turned out to be exactly the right combination for a year that made no other kind of sense.

The motorhome was not large. This became immediately clear when Taty tried to stand up in the back and hit the roof, and clear again when the girls discovered the dining table folded into a bed and spent the next hour folding and unfolding it while we tried to drive through Hardangerfjord. Norway’s roads near the fjords were designed by people who did not envision motorhomes. We scraped exactly one fence. It was not our fence.

At night, parked above a fjord with the water three hundred metres below and the sky still light at midnight — this is what Norway does to you. It makes small problems feel very small and large problems temporarily irrelevant.

What the motorhome gave us that no hotel ever could: the ability to wake up exactly where we fell asleep — which in Norway means waking up inside the landscape rather than adjacent to it. Breakfast with a fjord view costs nothing if you drove there last night. The girls ate cereal looking out at Geirangerfjord, which is a UNESCO World Heritage site, as if this were completely ordinary. After three days, it started to feel completely ordinary.

That normalisation of extraordinary things might be the most valuable thing travel does for children. Geiranger. Trollstigen. Flam. The Aurlandsfjord. Each one more impossible than the last, each one absorbed by the girls with the calm certainty that this is simply what the world looks like — that beauty on this scale is not the exception but the baseline. We hope that sticks.

Chapter 05

Argentina
Varados

Buenos Aires · Patagonia 2020 — Stranded 493 Photos

We landed in Buenos Aires in March 2020 to visit family, and then the world closed. The flights back to Oslo disappeared one by one over ten days until there were none. Argentina’s border shut. Norway’s border shut. We were stranded in the most beautiful way it is possible to be stranded: surrounded by Malena’s family, in a city that feeds you at every corner, with nowhere to be and, suddenly, no plan at all.

For children, the word “stranded” loses its panic entirely when it means more time with grandparents. More time in the warm apartment in Palermo that always smells of medialunas. More afternoons in the Botanical Garden, where the cats outnumber the plants and the girls considered this a design success. Buenos Aires in pandemic-adjacent March was strange and quiet and kind — fewer people on the streets, but the people who were there more present, more willing to talk.

“Being lost here is not the worst thing,” Taty said, looking at the city from a rooftop in San Telmo on a warm autumn evening. It was not the worst thing at all.

When the domestic flights reopened briefly, we drove south. Patagonia is a place that makes you reconsider everything you thought you understood about scale. The steppe goes on indefinitely. The wind is a physical force. The mountains, when they finally appear — the jagged drama of the Fitz Roy range — are not what you expected because nothing prepares you for how sharp they are against the sky. The girls went quiet for twenty minutes, which is unprecedented.

We were in Argentina for four months in the end. By the time we finally flew back to Oslo, the girls’ Spanish had improved enormously, they had a new set of cousins who felt like old friends, and Amelie had developed a position on empanadas (correct: Salta-style with egg and potato; wrong: anything else) that she defends to this day. Being stranded taught us something about the difference between a trip and a stay, between visiting a place and temporarily belonging to it.

Chapter 06

Hytte
Weekend

Norwegian Mountains · Cabin Life May 2025 119 Photos

Not every trip has to be a continent. This is something we had to learn, coming from a family culture that measures adventure in flight hours and passport stamps. Norway taught us the other kind — the weekend in the mountains, the cabin without wifi, the season changing so dramatically in a single week that you feel the planet actually rotating. A hytte weekend in Norway is not a lesser version of a trip. It is its own category entirely.

May in the Norwegian mountains is a negotiation between winter and spring that winter hasn’t quite conceded yet. There was still snow on the ground when we arrived — heavy, wet May snow that the girls immediately ran into without waterproofs — and by Sunday afternoon the sun had appeared with such intensity that we ate lunch outside in short sleeves, surrounded by snowdrifts. This is a normal Norwegian weekend. Norwegians are unbothered by it. We find it spectacular every time.

The cabin had a wood-burning stove, a stack of old board games with missing pieces, and a view of a frozen lake that thawed, incrementally, over the course of the weekend. Lara went fishing. She caught nothing, which she considered irrelevant to the experience. Amelie built a snow fort she defended with great seriousness. Malena read an entire book. Taty knitted. This is what rest looks like.

Chapter 07

Cape
Town

South Africa · Cape Peninsula 168 Photos

Table Mountain does something to a city. Cape Town has the same basic ingredients as dozens of other coastal cities — harbour, wine country, beach suburb — but Table Mountain changes all of it. It sits above everything, flat-topped and vast, a presence rather than just a backdrop. You can see it from the airport, from the waterfront, from the highway, from your hotel window at 6am when you’re jetlagged and standing there drinking bad coffee, trying to understand why you feel, already, that you’ve been here before.

The penguins at Boulders Beach were, for the girls, the reason we came and the defining fact of southern Africa. African penguins waddled between the boardwalk and the water in complete indifference to thirty tourists photographing them from close range. Amelie crouched next to one for a photo. The penguin regarded her with what appeared to be mild professional contempt. This is the correct energy.

Cape Point, at the bottom of the peninsula, is where the Indian and Atlantic Oceans meet. The wind says something more urgent than any sign. We stood on the cliff and couldn’t hear each other speak and didn’t need to.

The winelands around Franschhoek produced the best lunch of the trip — a long, slow affair at a farm table under oak trees, where the children ate and ran and came back and ate again, and the adults had the very good idea of ordering a second bottle of something cold and local, and nobody rushed anything. These are the meals that feel, afterward, like they contained more time than they actually did.
